Chile is the longest country in the world. You probably knew that. What you don't fully understand until you're in it is what that actually means for geography, climate, and connectivity.

The Atacama Desert in the north is one of the driest places on earth. The central valley around Santiago and Valparaíso is Mediterranean and very liveable. The lake district south of Temuco is green and volcanic and reminds me of New Zealand in a way I couldn't quite explain. Then Patagonia: Torres del Paine, where the wind is genuinely violent and the mountains do that thing where they look fake because no real thing could actually look like that.

I've done the Atacama (San Pedro de Atacama, specifically, which earns its reputation), Santiago more than once, a long drive down the Carretera Austral in a rented car with dubious suspension, and Torres del Paine in what I'm told was light wind (it did not feel like light wind).

Chile's coverage follows the population, which means good in Santiago and the main cities, solid along the main highways, and variable to absent in the national parks and Patagonian wilderness. Entel in particular has been aggressive about rural coverage and is often the strongest option outside the cities.

Here are the five best eSIMs for Chile in 2026.

## How to Choose the Right eSIM for Chile

**Chile is long. Your connectivity needs change dramatically with latitude.** Santiago: full urban 4G. Atacama: solid in San Pedro, patchy on excursions. Lake district: good in towns, less so between them. Patagonia: covered in gateway towns (Puerto Natales, Punta Arenas), minimal in the parks. The Carretera Austral: covered at towns along the route, gaps in between. Plan your data budget around where you'll actually have signal.

**Entel matters here.** Entel is consistently rated Chile's best network for rural and highland coverage. Any eSIM that connects to Entel is at an advantage for Chile's more remote destinations. eSIMply connects to Entel as part of its network combination, which is part of why it's my pick for Chile specifically.

**Patagonia offline prep is non-negotiable.** Torres del Paine and the Carretera Austral routes require offline maps, downloaded guides, and accommodation confirmations downloaded before you leave covered areas. Do this in Santiago or Puerto Natales. Signal in the parks is absent on most trails. This isn't an eSIM issue, it's a Chile infrastructure issue.

**How long is your trip and where are you going?** A week in Santiago and Valparaíso: 3GB is comfortable. Three weeks covering Santiago, Atacama, and Patagonia: 5GB is right, because you'll have significant time without signal in the national parks. A month including remote work from Santiago: 10GB.

**Chile plus Argentina (very common in Patagonia)?** Many Patagonia trips cross the Chile/Argentina border, particularly routes through Torres del Paine from El Calafate on the Argentine side. If your trip involves significant time in Argentine Patagonia, factor in a separate Argentine eSIM or a regional/multi-country approach.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do eSIMs work well in Chile?**

Yes, in cities and along main tourist corridors. Entel, Movistar, and Claro have strong 4G coverage across Santiago, Valparaíso, and major urban areas. Coverage along the Atacama routes is solid near San Pedro. The Carretera Austral and Patagonian national parks have coverage at towns and key stops, with significant gaps in backcountry areas. For city and main-route travel, eSIMs work reliably.

**Is an eSIM cheaper than roaming in Chile?**

Yes, significantly. International roaming in Chile from a European or Australian plan typically costs $8-20 per day. A 3GB eSIM from eSIMply for 30 days costs $8.95 total. The comparison isn't close. Buy an eSIM.

**Which Chilean network has the best coverage?**

Entel is consistently rated as Chile's best network for overall coverage, particularly in rural areas and remote routes like the Carretera Austral. Movistar and Claro are strong in Santiago and urban areas. For a Chile trip that includes any time off the main highways, an eSIM that connects to Entel is a meaningful advantage.

**Will my eSIM work in the Atacama Desert?**

In San Pedro de Atacama town: yes, solid coverage. On excursions into the desert (Valle de la Luna, El Tatio geysers, Lagunas Altiplanicas): coverage varies and can drop significantly away from the road. Download offline maps for the Atacama region before leaving San Pedro. Signal comes back reliably in town.

**Does my eSIM work in Torres del Paine?**

In Puerto Natales (the main gateway town): yes. Near the park entrance and at the main visitor areas: some signal. On the trails, including the W Trek and the Circuit: minimal to no signal on most of the route. You are genuinely offline in Torres del Paine when hiking. Download trail maps, offline guides, and booking confirmations before you leave Puerto Natales. This is one of those situations where the wilderness genuinely wins.

**How do I set up a Chile eSIM?**

Buy online before you travel. The provider sends a QR code. Go to your phone settings, add eSIM, scan the code, set activation for when you land. Confirm your phone is eSIM-compatible and not carrier-locked. Most phones made after 2018 are eSIM-ready.

**How much data do I need for Chile?**

1-2GB for a week in Santiago with Wi-Fi at accommodation. 3-5GB for two to three weeks including Atacama and Patagonia, accounting for the fact that you'll have zero data use on trail days. 10GB for a month of remote work in Santiago between wilderness trips. The Patagonia reality is that you'll burn less data than expected in the parks, but you want a solid buffer for when you're back in town with signal.

**Can I stream Netflix in Chile on my eSIM?**

In Santiago, yes, easily. The 4G data speeds in the city are solid enough for streaming. In Patagonia, you're mostly offline on the trails, so Netflix is a Santiago and Puerto Natales evening activity. Budget the data accordingly.

**Is the Carretera Austral worth the connectivity headache?**

Completely. The Carretera Austral is one of the most spectacular drives in the world and the intermittent connectivity is part of the experience rather than a bug. Towns like Futaleufú, Cochrane, and Puerto Guadal have coverage. The stretches between them, the lake viewpoints, the hanging glaciers: you're offline, and the correct response to this is to put your phone away.
